I. The Learners' Reality
Personal Anecdotes
  • Award Winner Breakouts:

At the end of our annual conference, we had a separate call with Award Winners that included breakouts with representatives from our org. One significant, consistent theme emerged as representatives debriefed those conversations: AI. Some just wanted to discuss its emergence in our sphere, others explained how they and others use it, others had questions. One rep shared that when she asked what was actually being sought, the Award Winner admitted they don't know — and they don't think the people asking know either. Big gap. All but 1 or 2 org reps raised their hands in the debrief when asked if AI was brought up. 3 sentiments that have stuck with me since:

1. "I can't sell a solution (to stakeholders) that doesn't include AI in some capacity."
2. "When are you all going to join us in the 21st century?"
3. "Why attend intensive trainings, when I can use AI to help me plan a facilitation?"
  • Leadership course | Don't get caught flat-footed

When a learner in a live session raised the question of whether our org had AI resources or support to offer, the facilitator didn't have much to give — she could confirm there wasn't one, and indicated she could check. Not a knock on her. It's exactly what the title says.

"...oh and we've got [learner] asking about AI 😳 oh boy...it's coming..."— Support staff, live session breakout, 2026

A learner in our leadership course was already using AI to code and category assessment and behavior data. He asked about it during class, followed up 1:1, and offered to share his prompts. He wasn't asking for permission — he was checking whether the org could be a resource for what he was already doing.

Lesson: That question doesn't go away unanswered, and as time goes on it will almost certainly increase. AI Fluency is beginning to prepare to properly handle these conversations beyond policies, particularly ones not firmly set.

II. Keep Content Alive and Robust
  • Honor the Work

Our ED Dev team pours time, care, and craft into every project. Real AI fluency means understanding how proper utilization honors that work — not waters it down. But you have to be fluent enough to see the full picture first, and then contribute to it meaningfully. That's the point. When dormant content gets a second life — when a deep-dive module becomes a practitioner post or a sharp graphic — the work isn't forgotten. It's amplified. This is the upside no one talks about: repurposing is respect.

Foundations: The Train that Could 🚂

Disillusionment. That was the palpable sentiment throughout the project and during the debrief by the people most heavily involved. There was a clear disconnect between Brand/Product's messaging and public hype, and what learners actually needed — the purpose of the activity, and ultimately the deliverable. While the work itself was quality and catered well to learner needs, the disconnect made planning and organizing difficult throughout. Many noted the scope felt like it had changed multiple times. By the end, it felt like a different thing entirely. The result: disillusionment at the top, followed by dormancy. We saw something similar with the upgrade course a few years ago.

  • The Cycle: Build → Place → Consume → Dormant

This is our current content cycle, aside from occasional updates. We develop quality content that gets consumed and well-received — but we have significant opportunity to both honor dormant work and increase volume and speed of output. The name of the game in content consumption today is quick and engaging. Our practitioner message board lends itself to exactly this: small pieces of dormant content pulled for engagement posts, quick AI-generated graphics — especially fast with tools like Claude design where branding and style are already loaded.

Proper AI utilization adds a step before the inevitable dormant phase: Repurpose. That repurposing translates directly to the quick and engaging format today's world demands — and it honors the work done with blood, sweat, and tears. One of the disillusioned Foundations members — a pivotal one — is gone. Obviously that wasn't the reason. But there is an emotional toll when the dormant phase arrives faster than it should.

III. The Bottleneck Factor

True AI Fluency unlocks a seemingly endless stream of ideas. You — yes, you, the reader — have had so many ideas over the years that had nowhere to go. It would require looping in too many people. It needs higher-level approval. It calls for cross-department collaboration. It would take months. When you finally unlock the stream of ideas, you begin to notice the removal of many of these barriers — and the mental barriers that come along with them. Think of some of your own ideas that never gained traction because of those barriers. Now imagine a world where some of those can be made possible, or at least prototyped for proof-of-concept, within minutes and hours instead of months and years.

Ideas requiring anything beyond standard templates had a ceiling. Reaching it meant the UX queue: approval chains, resource allocation, timelines measured in quarters. Most ideas died in the backlog — not because they were bad, but because the execution cost was prohibitive.

That bottleneck moved. What took a quarter now takes an afternoon. This isn't about one role or one team — it's a structural shift across every function where ideas die in queues.

"(joking) Are you saying you can vibe code better than our UX team?" — Teammate reaction to an early prototype
No, I can't. And it's not the point. The point is that ideas that were non-starters because of that level of work and coordination can now be reduced to a conversation or chat session.

Unlocking the stream of ideas is only one component. The other is time. Another key constraint for any idea is the time factor, particularly if it includes multiple people. Not only does the unlocking allow for more ideas, it allows for both more time to execute those ideas, and increased flexibility regarding planning projects. With AI Fluency and proper utilization, both our capabilities and the subsequent possibilities increase dramatically.

Certain things no longer require the queue at all.

IV. Small and Subtle

AI is already part of the workplace conversations our facilitators are walking into. Important discussions are happening in every org and industry. But where is our team's anecdotal data on what that looks like for our learners?

AI fluency adoption is meant to be slow, subtle, and on each person's own terms. It doesn't need to be announced. It doesn't need a rollout. It shows itself the same way it showed up in that leadership course — a learner, already using it, just wanting to know if we're paying attention.

Three Dimensions of Sustainable AI Transformation

The goal isn't to move everyone at once. It's to create conditions where people can meet it from their own vantage point, ask their own questions, and build their own relationship with it — at the pace that fits them.

"It's not just about fancy tools, it's about culture, processes, structure...The tech is important, but it's how you use it that matters. Organizations that succeed with AI are the ones that combine great data with a culture of experimentation and learning."— University of Maryland
